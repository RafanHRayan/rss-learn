# Circles

## Defining a Circle

A circle is commonly known to many, yet it is much more complex.

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> Definition of A Circle</summary>

A circle is the set of all points equidistant from a center point.

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> Definition of A Center</summary>

A point is the center of a circle if and only if it is the point in which all the points of a circle are equidistant.

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> Definition of A Radius</summary>

A segment is the radius of a circle if and only if it is the distance in which every point of a circle is from the center.

</details>

## Pi

**Pi** ($$\pi{}$$) is a mathematical concept that is equivalent to the ratio of a circle's circumfrance to its diameter.

Assuming that $$r$$ is the radius of any given circle, we can conclude that the circumfrance of a circle is $$2\pi{}r$$ and the area of a circle is $$\pi{}r^2$$.

## Radians

**Radians** are another way to reference angle and arc measures, being a popular alternative to degrees. A radian is the ratio of the arc length to the radius. There are $$2\pi{}$$ radians in a circle, meaing that $$180\degree{}=\pi{}\text{ rad}$$.

Because of this, it is easy to convert between degrees and radians using ratios...

$$
\frac{1}{180}=\frac{r}{d}
$$

In this proportion, $$d$$ is the degree measure you want to convert to and $$r$$ is the measure in radians in terms of $$\pi{}$$.

{% hint style="info" icon="vial-vertical" %}
If we want to convert 120° into radians, we would solve the proportion for it: $$\frac{1}{180}=\frac{r}{120}$$. Solving the proportion would yield that $$r=\frac{2}{3}$$, which means that $$120\degree{}=\frac{2\pi{}}{3}\text{ rad}$$.
{% endhint %}

## Fundamental Theorems

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> Radius Congruence</summary>

All radii of the same circle are congruent.

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> Tangent Perpendicularity</summary>

A tangent to a circle is always perpendicular to the radius that meets its point of tangency.

{% hint style="success" icon="fire-flame-curved" %}
A tangent is called such because of its relation to the [tangent function](../trigonometry/trigonometry-in-right-triangles.md#basic-trigonometric-functions). Let's assume that we have a circle, $$O$$, and point $$O$$ connects to a point, $$P$$, which is on the circle; the radius of the circle is 1; a point, $$Q$$, is some other point on the tangent; the tangent is $$\overleftrightarrow{QP}$$; and the angle with the vertex, $$O$$ ($$\angle{QOP}$$), has a measure of $$\theta{}$$.

With this, we can conclude that $$\tan{\theta{}}=QP$$.
{% endhint %}

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> Tangent Congruence</summary>

If two segments are drawn tangent to the same circle and meet at a common point outside of the circle, then the lengths of both tangents are congruent.

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> Central Angle Congruence</summary>

If a central angle is drawn in a circle, then its measure is equal to the measure of the intercepted arc and any other central angle intercepting the same arc.

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> Inscribed Angle Theorem</summary>

If an inscribed angle is drawn in a circle, then its measure is half the measure of the intercepted arc.

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> Thales' Theorem</summary>

If an inscribed angle intercepts a semicircle, then it is a right angle.

</details>

## Advanced Theorems

### Chord Theorems

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> <strong>Chord Theorem #1</strong> — Chord Congruence</summary>

If two chords are congruent, then their intercepted arcs are equal in measure.

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> <strong>Chord Theorem #2</strong> — Chord Parallelism</summary>

If two chords are parallel, then [the arcs between them](#user-content-fn-1)[^1] are equal in measure.

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> <strong>Chord Theorem #3</strong> — Chord Perpendicularity</summary>

If a diameter is perpendicular to a chord, then it bisects the chord and the arc that the chord intercepts.

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> <strong>Chord Theorem #4</strong> — Chord-Tangent Angle</summary>

If a tangent and a chord form an angle, then the angle's measure is half the measure of the arc the chord intercepts.

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> <strong>Chord Theorem #5</strong> — Chord-Chord Vertical Angles</summary>

If vertical angles are formed by the intersection of two chords, then their measures are the [arithmetic mean](#user-content-fn-2)[^2] of the measures of the arcs they intercept.

</details>

### Tangent-Secant Theorems

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> <strong>Tangent-Secant Theorem #1</strong> — Secant-Secant Exterior Angle</summary>

If two secants form and angle outside a circle, then its measure is half the positive difference of the measures of the arcs intercepted by the secants.

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> <strong>Tangent-Secant Theorem #2</strong> — Secant-Tangent Exterior Angle</summary>

If a secant and a tangent form and angle outside a circle, then its measure is half the positive difference of the measures of the arcs intercepted by the secant and the tangent.

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> <strong>Tangent-Secant Theorem #3</strong> — Tangent-Tangent Exterior Angle</summary>

If two tangents form and angle outside a circle, then its measure is half the positive difference of the measures of the arcs intercepted by the tangents.

</details>

### Power of A Point Theorem

The Power of a Point theorem proves the following cases...

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> <strong>Power of A Point Case #1</strong> — Chord-Chord Interception</summary>

If two chords meet inside a circle, then the products of their respective parts are equivalent.

{% hint style="info" icon="circle-question" %}
If $$\overline{AB}$$ and $$\overline{CD}$$ are chords that meet inside a circle at a point, $$X$$, then $$AX*XB=CX*XD$$.
{% endhint %}

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> <strong>Power of A Point Case #2</strong> — Secant-Secant Interception</summary>

If two secants meet outside a circle, then the products of their respective outside parts and whole lengths are equivalent.

{% hint style="info" icon="circle-question" %}
If $$\overline{AC}$$ and $$\overline{AY}$$ are secants that meet outside a circle at point, $$A$$, and each individually meet the circle at points, $$B$$ and $$C$$, and $$X$$ and $$Y$$ respectively, then $$AB*AC=AX*AY$$.
{% endhint %}

</details>

<details>

<summary><i class="fa-note">:note:</i> <strong>Power of A Point Case #3</strong> — Secant-Tangent Interception</summary>

If a secant and a tangent meet outside a circle, then the length of the tangent segment is the geometric mean of the outside part and whole length of the secant segment.

{% hint style="info" icon="circle-question" %}
If $$\overline{AB}$$ is a tangent; $$\overline{AD}$$ is a secant; both meet outside a circle at point, $$A$$; the tangent meets the circle at $$B$$; and the secant meets the circle at $$C$$ and $$D$$; then $$\frac{AB}{AC}=\frac{AD}{AB}$$.
{% endhint %}

</details>

[^1]: The arcs in between two chords reference the two split arcs that _aren't_ the arcs intercepted by the chords.

[^2]: The average.
