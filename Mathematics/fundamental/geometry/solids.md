# Solids

For this point on, two-dimensional figures have been the main focus, but if we look at another axis, we can find more complex ideas that use applications from two-dimensional figures.

## Defining Solids

A **solid** is a three-dimensional figure.

### Basic Solid Terminology

A **face** of a solid is a flat, two-dimensional surface that forms part of the boundary of a solid object.

An **edge** of a solid is a straight line segment where two faces of a polyhedron meet. An edge can also be curved.

A **vertex** of a solid is a point where three or more edges meet.

A **base** of a solid the face upon which the solid is oriented. A solid can have multiple bases.

A **lateral face** of a solid is a face that is not a base.

A **lateral edge** of a solid is the edge formed by the intersection of two adjacent lateral faces of a polyhedron.

**Volume** is the amount of space that a solid takes up. It is essentially how many 1x1x1 cubes can fit inside the solid.

### Types of Solids

There are several types of solids...

* A solid is a **polyhedron** if and only if it is bounded by flat polygonal surfaces which intersect at edges and vertices.
  * A polyhedron is a **prism** if and only if it has two congruent, parallel bases and parallelogram lateral faces joining the bases.
  * A polyhedron is a **pyramid** if and only if it has triangular lateral faces joining a single base to a single vertex.
* A solid is a **cylinder** if and only if has two congruent, parallel, non-polygonal bases joined by a single lateral face.
* A solid is a **cone** if and only if it is bounded by one non-polygonal base and a single lateral face that tapers to a vertex.
* A solid is a **sphere** if and only if it is the set of all points equidistant from a fixed point.

## Polyhedrons

### Prisms

A prism is **right** if and only if its lateral edges are parallel to its bases. Otherwise, it is **oblique**.

In a right prism, every lateral edge is an altitude. This is the opposite in an oblique prism.

A prism is **regular** if its bases are regular.

The volume of a prism is found using the formula $$V=Bh$$, where $$B$$ is the area of each of the bases and $$h$$ is the height.

### Pyramids

The **apex** of a pyramid is the point where all the lateral faces of the pyramid join together.

A pyramid is **right** if and only if its altitude meets its base at its center. Otherwise, it is **oblique**.

A pyramid is **regular** if its base is regular.

Most right pyramids are also regular because their bases must have a center. One exception is [the rectangle](#user-content-fn-1)[^1], which is irregular but has a center.

A length is the **slant height** of a right regular pyramid if and only if it is the distance from the midpoint of a base edge edge to the apex. In the case that the pyramid is not right and/or regular, there will be multiple unique slant heights in the pyramid.

The volume of a prism is found using the formula $$V=\frac{1}{3}Bh$$, where $$B$$ is the area of each of the bases and $$h$$ is the height.

## Curved Solids

### Cylinders

A cylinder is **right** if and only if an altitude from a point on one base meets its corresponding point on the other. Otherwise, it is **oblique**.

A cylinder is **circular** if its bases are circles.

The volume of a prism is found using the formula $$V=Bh$$, where $$B$$ is the area of each of the bases and $$h$$ is the height.

### Cone

The **apex** of a cone is the point where the lateral face tapers to.

A cone is **right** if and only if its altitude meets its base at its center. Otherwise, it is **oblique**.

A cone is **circular** if its base is a circle.

A length is the **slant height** of a right circular cone if and only if it is the distance from any point on the circumference of the base to the apex. In the case that the pyramid is not right and/or circular, there will be an infinite amount of unique slant heights in the cone.

The volume of a prism is found using the formula $$V=\frac{1}{3}Bh$$, where $$B$$ is the area of each of the bases and $$h$$ is the height.

### Spheres

The **center** of a sphere is the fixed point in which all points on the sphere are equidistant from.

The **radius** of a sphere is the distance from the center to any point on the sphere.

The volume of a prism is found using the formula $$V=\frac{4}{3}\pi{}r^3$$, where $$r$$ is the radius.

## Cross-Sections

A plane figure is a **cross-section** of a solid if and only if it is formed by the intersection of the solid and plane.

Cross-sections parallel to the base will always be the same shape as the base.

For cross-sections perpendicular to the base of its solid...

* Cross-sections perpendicular to the base of a _prism_ or _cylinder_ will always be a rectangle.
* Cross-sections perpendicular to the base of a _pyramid_ will always be a triangle.
* Cross-sections perpendicular to the base of a _cone_ will always be a triangle if and only if the plane passes through the apex.

In a sphere—which has no base—all cross-sections will be a circle.

## Solids of Revolution

Solids can be formed by rotating specific shapes around an axis, called the **axis of revolution**.

The solid formed by rotating a _**rectangle**_ around by any _side_ is a cylinder, with the radius of the base being the the length of either side perpendicular to the axis of revolution and the height being the length of the side parallel to the axis of revolution.

### Solids of Revolution With Empty Space

Sometimes, a solid will have empty space. When this happens, the solid formed will be the solid formed by the shape of the entire filled and empty space with a depression of the solid that is made from the empty space.

## Cavalieri's Principle

**Cavalieri's Principle** states that if two solids with coplanar bases have the same height and the same cross-section in every plane parallel to their bases, then they have the same volume.

This is why we can say that the volume of an oblique solid is the same as the volume of the same solid if it was right.

A plane figure is a **net** of a solid if and only if it may be folded to form that solid.
