## The Ming Dynasty

### Fall of the Yuan

After the Yuan Dynasty's reign ended under Mongol rule after the death of Kublai Khan in 1294, the Yuan Dynasty weakened.

The Chinese resented Mongol rule, as Confucianism not respected. This led to rebellions. Ultimately, China was once again unified under the Ming Dynasty in 1368.

### Rise of The Ming Dynasty

The Ming Dynasty was ruled from 1368-1398 by Emperor Hongwu.

Hongwu rebuilt the strong central government from before and reinstated civil service examinations, making them even more difficult.

### The Forbidden City

Emperor Zhu Di, who ruled from 1402-1425, began to make major changes to China. He created the Forbidden City: 178 acres of of private housing for Ming and Qing emperors from 1420 all the way 1911.

### Zheng He's Fleet

Emperor Zhu Di desired to send ships to the west of China.

Zheng He—born to a Muslim family—was placed as the admiral in control of the huge fleet and armed forces that undertook the expeditions. But, these were not strictly explorations. He was tasked to do this in order to receive tribute from other places.

Despite trade being important in the voyage, the main mission was collecting tribute; Zheng He had over 25,000 soldiers aboard his fleet.]

These journeys occurred over the course of decades. The economic motive for these huge ventures may have been important, but the chief aim was probably political: to mark the reemergence of the Chinese Empire following nearly a century of Mongol rule.

From the ninth century on, the Chinese had taken their magnetic compasses aboard ships to use for navigating (two centuries before Europe). The Chinese could navigate by the stars using printed manuals with star charts. Because the emperor wanted to impress Ming power upon the world and show off China's resources and importance, he gave orders to build even larger ships than were necessary for the voyages. Thus the word went out to construct special "Treasure Ships”.

### Fall of The Ming's Maritime Power

However, because of the expenses of these voyages and ships, the whole plan was shut down, in favor of traditional policies of isolation from the rest of the world. The Ming now heavily restricted foreign trade and travel and foreign merchants were allowed to trade only at few ports, during certain times.
