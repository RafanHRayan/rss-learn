# The Gunpowder Empires

Around the 1300s, [three powerful empires](#user-content-fn-1)[^1]—fittingly named the Gunpowder Empires—began to rise in power for their use of gunpowder in cannons.

## The Mughal Empire

The Mughal Empire was once such empire, ruling over the Indian region from 1526-1707.

### Babur The Tiger

A descendant of Genghis Khan himself, Babur the Tiger launched an invasion into India from the North in 1520.

The Mughal army of about 12,000 beat an army ten times its size because of a modern arsenary they wielded: Babur the Tiger took Genghis Khan's horse and archer tactics and combined with new gunpowder weapons.

### Akbar

Babur died from health problems, after smoking and drinking too much, and his son died after falling down the stairs. :laughing:

Thus, Akbar—Babur's grandson—rose up to the throne at the young age of 13. He ruled from 1556-1605. Akbar immediately faced threats from Mughal enemies and he defeated them.

### The Problem of Ruling India

India was a majorly Hindu population, and Akbar was Muslim. Akbar perceived that three things were needed if his Empire was to be stable and long-lasting...

* Fair rent.
* Impartial rule.
* Religious tolerance.

> "As most men are fettered by bonds of tradition, and by imitating ways followed by their fathers…everyone continues, without investigating their arguments and reasons, to follow the religion in which he was born and educated, thus excluding himself from the possibility of ascertaining the truth, which is the noblest aim of the human intellect. Therefore, we associate at convenient seasons with learned men of all religions, thus deriving profits from their exquisite discourses and exalted aspirations."
>
> — Akbar the Great, 1582 (letter to the king of Spain)

In order to keep the empire happy, Akbar ended the jizya[^2], promoted Hindus into the government, and allowed Hindu temples to be built. Often, he met with Catholic priests.

Akbar took ideas from Islam, Hinduism, Christianity, and Zoroastrianism and blended them into the Divine Faith. Akbar hoped the Divine Faith would end conflicts between Muslims & Hindus.

The Divine Faith was an example of syncretism, the blending of religions.

Additionally, Akbar created the [Ibadat Khana](#user-content-fn-3)[^3]. In this place, Akbar sponsored debate and discussions between religious leaders.

Akbar also have social tolerance. He prohibited sati, which is when upper-caste widowed women would throw themselves on the funeral pyre of their husband.

Akbar also established a ritual whereby every morning he appeared before his people at a special window on the “balcony for viewing.” He presented himself as “the illuminator of the universe”. The sight of the emperor at dawn at a window high in the sky had parallels to his Hindu subjects’ reverence for the daily reappearance of the supreme celestial source of light and energy.

### Shah Jahan

During Shah Jahan's rule the status of women was higher in the court of the ruler. However, sati found its way back, unveiled women were shunned, and burden of dowry returned.

#### The Taj Mahal

Taj Mahal started in 1632. It was built for Shah Jahan’s wife Mumtaz Mahal, who died in childbirth. It has come to be known as a symbol of love, but it’s a mausoleum. But, this building was so expensive that Jahan emptied the treasury and he was forced to heavily tax the people to have it completed.

After Shah Jahan's death, the Taj Mahal also became his burial place.

### Aurangzeb

After Shah Jahan fell ill, his sons fought for power. Aurangzeb was the one who rose to power. After Shah Jahan recovered, Aurangzeb confined his father, to his own palace in Agra.

Aurangzeb was more orthodox and austere than his predecessors, and his increased religiosity is reflected in the artworks produced during his reign. He has two goals...

1. Extend Mughal control throughout the subcontinent.
2. Balance the budget.

Firstly, Aurangzeb banned music and restored the jizya. He also tore down the Hindu temples.

### Decline of the Mughal Empire

This results in rebellion, declining power of the Mughal Empire by the 1700s. Great Britain took advantage of this weakness, conquered India, and removed the last Mughal emperor from power in 1858.

## The Ottoman Empire

Around 1300, the Muslim Turks of Anatolia were unified and formed the Ottoman Empire after a man named Osman led a successful invasion of Anatolia. The Ottomans used muskets and cannons to form a powerful army and expand their territory.

### Expansion of The Ottoman Empire

Using cannons, the Ottoman Empire was able to breach the protective walls of Constantinople, finally seizing the land in 1453. This was done under the rule of Mehmet II. This was the final demise of Christian Byzantium. Ottoman rulers could now see themselves as successors of the Roman Empire.

The Ottomans then [convert the church of Hagia Sophia into a mosque](byzantine-empire.md#the-hagia-sophia).

### Effects of The Loss of Byzantium

After capturing Constantinople—and renaming it to what is now Istanbul—the Ottomans cut off Christian Europe from its major source of goods and resources. This forced Europeans to seek new routes to enter Asia. This completely disrupts the Silk Road.

Access to the spices, silks and luxury goods of the east was closed off to the west. New maritime trade routes would need to cultivated.

### Suleiman the Magnificent

Ruling from 1520-1566, Suleiman the Magnificent upgraded the Ottoman Empire. Through the use of his modernized military and diplomatic skill, he built one of the largest empires in Europe and the Middle East. Art, architecture, and poetry also flourished.

### The Devshirme System

Young Christian males between the ages of 8-15 were pulled from their families and trained in military techniques. Some become the elite infantry of the Ottoman Empire—the Janissary Corps. Their jobs were based on merit, and they were tasked with protecting Christians and Jews from mob violence.

> "I made my first acquaintance with the Janissaries; this is the name by which the Turks call the infantry \[foot soldiers] of the royal guard. The Turkish state has 12,000 of these troops... They are scattered through every part of the empire...to protect the Christians and Jews from the violence of the mob...
>
> — Ogier Ghiselin de Busbecq, writer and diplomat

### The Millet System

Millet System was a separate legal court pertaining to "personal law" under which a [confessional community](#user-content-fn-4)[^4] was allowed to rule itself under its own system.
